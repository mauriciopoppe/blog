# Writing Styleguide

This guide captures the authentic writing style of the author, based on a recursive analysis of their original notes. Follow these rules to maintain a consistent, human, and personal voice.

## General Tone

- **Simple and Direct**: Use plain, everyday language. Avoid academic, marketing-heavy, or "sophisticated" words. (e.g., avoid "obsess", "massive", "epiphany", "breakthrough", "leverage", "utilize", "comprehensive", "notoriously").
- **Personal and Narrative**: Write from a first-person perspective ("I", "my"). Technical topics should be grounded in personal curiosity, a specific project, or a hobby.
- **Human and Humble**: It's okay to admit being new to a topic, making mistakes, or having an imperfect setup. Avoid sounding like an "authority" or an omniscient expert.
- **Colloquial and Informal**: Use conversational phrases like "It's alright", "That's ok", "Anyway", "I guess", "Which is nice", "First and foremost", "For these reasons", "So the short answer... is X", or "I realized that".

## Vocabulary and Phrasing

- **Prefer Common Synonyms**:
  - "Big" or "a lot" instead of "massive" or "extensive".
  - "Use" instead of "leverage" or "utilize".
  - "Fix" or "correct these problems" instead of "implement a solution for".
  - "Learn" or "figure out" instead of "master" or "attain proficiency".
- **Colloquial Fillers**: Use "way more," "way too deep," "pretty cool," or "super cool."
- **Common Mistakes/Artifacts (DO NOT FIX)**:
  - **Run-on Sentences**: Linking multiple clauses with commas or "and" (e.g., "I went to all the classes and initially, I focused on...").
  - **Missing Articles**: Occasional missing "the" or "a" (e.g., "instead spend my time exploring...").
  - **Awkward Prepositions**: Using "in" instead of "on" or "at" (e.g., "practicing in the University of Valladolid problems").
  - **Informal Transitioning**: Starting sentences with "So," "Then," or "But."
  - **Repetition for Emphasis**: "It's just very, very fun."

## Punctuation and Symbols

- **No Semicolons**: Never use the `;` character. Use commas or split sentences into two.
- **No Em-Dashes**: Avoid the large dash (`—`). Use commas, periods, or simple hyphens (`-`).
- **Standard Quotes**: Use standard ASCII single quotes (`'`) and double quotes (`"`). Avoid "curly" or "smart" quotes.
- **Simple Lists**: Use simple dashes `-` for bullet points.
- **Parenthetical Asides**: Use `(parentheses)` for quick side notes or "bonus" info.

## Structure and Formatting

- **The Narrative Hook**: Technical articles should ideally start with a personal story (e.g., "I spent a few weeks using X", "I remember the first time I heard Y").
- **Descriptive, Low-Drama Headers**: Use simple headers like "How?", "Why?", "My process", "The Verdict", or "Before you start". Avoid dramatic or "hooky" titles.
- **Contextual Anchors**: Connect technical deep dives to why it matters to you *fundamentally* (e.g., "Learning how they work under the hood... is a great fundamental skill to have").
- **Visuals**: Use `full-screen-with-background` shortcodes for powerful quotes and `figure` for diagrams.
- **External Links**: Link to videos, maps, and specific references you admire (e.g., "vlogger that I admire called Riza").
